Core purpose:
- The paper’s main aim is to make discussion of AGI more precise and less rhetorical.
- It argues that “AGI” is often used too loosely, so researchers should separate:
  1) how broad a system’s capabilities are, and
  2) how strong its performance is relative to humans.

Central idea:
- The paper proposes a framework for classifying AI systems by levels rather than treating AGI as a single binary threshold.
- The framework is meant to operationalize progress toward AGI in a way that is more measurable and comparable.

Key structure of the framework:
- Breadth/generalization dimension:
  - Distinguishes narrow systems from more general systems.
  - A genuinely more general system can perform across many tasks or domains, especially novel ones, rather than excelling only in a single benchmark or modality.
- Performance level dimension:
  - Distinguishes systems by how well they perform compared with humans.

- This avoids calling a system “AGI” merely because it shows some flexibility while still being weak or brittle.

2. Nick Bostrom, Superintelligence

Core definition:
- Bostrom’s widely cited definition is that superintelligence is an intellect that greatly exceeds human cognitive performance in virtually all domains of interest.

What is doing the work in that definition:
- “Greatly exceeds human cognitive performance”:
  - Superintelligence is not just slightly better than humans; it is substantially above the human range.
- “Virtually all domains of interest”:
  - The superiority must be broad, not narrow.
  - This excludes systems that are merely extraordinary in one or a few specialized areas.
- “Intellect”:
  - The focus is on general cognitive power, not just speed, memory, or one benchmark result in isolation.

Implications of Bostrom’s definition:
- Breadth is essential:
  - A chess engine or protein-folding model would not qualify as superintelligence merely by being superhuman in its niche.
- Human comparison is the baseline:
  - The definition uses human cognitive performance as the reference point.
- The standard is domain-general competence:
  - “Virtually all domains of interest” implies near-comprehensive superiority across the kinds of tasks that matter for science, strategy, social interaction, planning, invention, and problem-solving.

How this relates to AGI:
- Bostrom’s definition is usually read as describing a level beyond AGI.

- So Bostrom’s definition is not mainly a definition of AGI itself; it is a definition of a stronger, post-AGI condition.
