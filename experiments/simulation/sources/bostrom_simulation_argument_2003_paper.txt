Nick Bostrom's 2003 paper introduces the Simulation Argument, which posits that at least one of three propositions is true: 

1. **The extinction of civilizations:** Almost all human-level civilizations go extinct before developing the capability for creating highly advanced simulations of reality (known as 'ancestor simulations').

2. **Lack of interest in running simulations:** Even if advanced civilizations achieve the technological capability, they may have little interest in creating ancestor simulations for various reasons such as ethical concerns or resource allocation.

3. **The high likelihood that we live in a simulation:** If (1) and (2) are false, it implies that a significant number of simulations are being run. Thus, it is statistically likely that we are presently living in a simulated reality rather than a base reality.

The argument rests on certain assumptions, such as the feasibility of creating simulations indistinguishable from reality and the technological potential for such developments, leading to what is known as the 'simulation hypothesis.'

Bostrom's argument does not definitively claim we live in a simulation but suggests a reevaluation of how we might consider our reality given these possibilities.
