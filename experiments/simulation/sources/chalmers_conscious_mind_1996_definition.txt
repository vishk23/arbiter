In "The Conscious Mind," David Chalmers explores the nature of consciousness and presents a dualistic approach, distinguishing between physical and phenomenal aspects. Chalmers introduces the concept of the "hard problem" of consciousness, which concerns why and how physical processes in the brain give rise to subjective experiences. He suggests that while physical processes can explain many aspects of consciousness, they fail to account for the subjective quality of experiences, which he refers to as "qualia."

Chalmers posits that consciousness cannot be reduced to physical explanations alone and proposes the idea of naturalistic dualism. He argues for the existence of fundamental properties and laws that include conscious experience as a basic component of the universe, similar to physical properties like mass and charge.

Chalmers also distinguishes between "easy problems" and the "hard problem" of consciousness. Easy problems involve explaining cognitive functions and behaviors, such as perception and information processing, which can be addressed by cognitive science and neuroscience. The hard problem, however, involves understanding the intrinsic nature of experience, which remains enigmatic.
