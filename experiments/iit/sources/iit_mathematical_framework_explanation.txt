Integrated Information Theory (IIT) provides a mathematical framework to assess consciousness by quantifying how integrated a system's information is. The fundamental premise is that consciousness corresponds to the capacity of a system to integrate information, forming a unified whole. This is measured by a value called \( \Phi \) (phi).

1. **Information Integration**: IIT posits that consciousness is determined by the ability of a system to integrate information across different parts. The degree of integration is quantified using \( \Phi \), representing the causally effective information that cannot be reduced to independent components.

2. **Conceptual Structure**: IIT defines a 'concept' as a specific state of information arrangement that contributes to consciousness. A conscious state is a set of "concepts" that exist due to the system's current state and its causal structure.

3. **System and Subsystems**: The theory evaluates the system at multiple levels, considering different subsystems and their ability to generate \( \Phi \). The most integrated subsystem providing the highest \( \Phi \) defines the level of consciousness.

4. **Exclusion Principle**: IIT introduces the exclusion principle, stating that consciousness corresponds to the set of concepts with the maximum \( \Phi \). This ensures that smaller subsets do not mistakenly attribute higher consciousness levels.

5. **Mathematical Characterization**: The framework involves complex mathematical formulations to quantify \( \Phi \) including measures like mutual information, effective information, and other information-theoretic metrics to evaluate how much the whole exceeds its parts.

The mathematical approach aims to rigorously define and quantify the phenomenon of consciousness, contributing to both neuroscientific research and theoretical philosophy.
