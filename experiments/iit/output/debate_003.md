The theory then derives postulates at the physical level, which C13 describes as generating empirically testable predictions.

Here lies the first critical gap. These are distinct epistemic levels and must not be conflated. The phenomenal axioms (C9) are established through introspection, insulated from falsification by their very framing as self-evident. The physical postulates are then *derived* from these irrefutable premises. This creates a methodological asymmetry: if the foundational axioms cannot be falsified, what exactly is being tested when C13's predictions are evaluated? A theory cannot simultaneously claim its foundations are irrefutable (C9) and that its derived predictions carry genuine empirical risk. Genuine falsifiability requires that disconfirming evidence can, in principle, threaten the core commitments—not merely peripheral predictions.

IIT defines a conscious system specifically as a *complex*: a maximal substrate possessing maximally irreducible cause-effect power. **Cross-Substrate Claims Without Cross-Substrate Tests**

The theory's mathematical framework cannot yet be fully applied to the very systems it purports to assess.

IIT 4.0, despite its formal sophistication, rests on axioms that are methodologically shielded from refutation while claiming downstream empirical testability. This is not a minor tension—it is a foundational challenge to its standing as an empirical science of consciousness.
