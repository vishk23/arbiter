========================================================================

Fischer and Ravizza defend an influential compatibilist theory of moral responsibility. Their central claim is that responsibility does not require metaphysical indeterminacy or a robust ability to do otherwise in every relevant case. What matters is whether the action issues from the agent’s own deliberative capacities in the right way.

The book distinguishes two kinds of control. **Regulative control** is the familiar power to choose or do otherwise. **Guidance control** is narrower: it concerns whether the actual action is being guided by the agent’s own mechanism for responding to reasons. Fischer and Ravizza argue that moral responsibility depends on guidance control, not on regulative control. This is why their view is often taken to support **semi-compatibilism**: even if determinism rules out alternative possibilities, responsibility may still remain intact.

A key concept is **reasons-responsiveness**. A mechanism is reasons-responsive when it is appropriately sensitive to reasons: if the agent had sufficient practical reasons to act differently, the mechanism would, in suitable nearby circumstances, lead the agent to respond differently. Fischer and Ravizza distinguish stronger and weaker forms of this idea. They do not require an idealized mechanism that would react correctly to every possible reason; rather, they argue that **moderate reasons-responsiveness** is enough for responsibility. The mechanism must not be merely accidental or inflexible, but it also need not be perfectly responsive in every counterfactual scenario.

They also emphasize that responsibility requires more than a functioning reasons-responsive mechanism in the abstract. The mechanism must be the agent’s own. For that, the agent must come to **take responsibility** for the mechanism. This historical condition is meant to explain ownership: through a suitable developmental process, the person comes to see themselves as an appropriate target of reactive attitudes such as praise, blame, resentment, and guilt. Once the agent takes responsibility for the relevant mechanism, actions issuing from it are attributable to the agent in the right way.

Their account is therefore both **mechanism-based** and **historical**. It is mechanism-based because the action must flow from a reasons-responsive process. It is historical because the agent must acquire ownership of that process through a proper path, not merely possess it at the moment of action. This helps them explain why some actions are not fully attributable even when they are voluntary: the mechanism may be distorted, bypassed, manipulated, or otherwise not truly the agent’s own.

The overall structure of the theory is: moral responsibility requires an action to issue from the agent’s own moderately reasons-responsive mechanism, and the agent must have taken responsibility for that mechanism. When those conditions are met, the agent exercises guidance control and is a proper subject of praise or blame, even if determinism is true.
