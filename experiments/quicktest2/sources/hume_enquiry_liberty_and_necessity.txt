Hume’s central claim in Section VIII is that human freedom and causal necessity are not enemies. He argues for a compatibilist view: a person is free when they can act or refrain from acting according to the determinations of their will, even if their choices and actions themselves have causes. What matters for liberty is not the absence of causation, but the absence of external constraint or compulsion.

Hume begins by redefining the two disputed terms. By “necessity,” he does not mean a hidden metaphysical force. He means the kind of causal regularity we infer everywhere in nature: when similar events are constantly conjoined, and from this we are able to predict one from the other. On this view, the behavior of human beings is part of the same causal order as physical events. Human actions regularly follow from motives, character, temperament, and circumstances, so we have as much reason to expect stable patterns in conduct as in the natural world.

He then argues that this sort of necessity is observable in ordinary life. We make predictions about people’s conduct based on experience, and these expectations underlie history, politics, law, and daily interaction. If human actions were not regular in this way, social life would be impossible. So necessity, understood as causal connection and inference from experience, is not only compatible with human action but presupposed by our practices.

Hume also distinguishes liberty from what he calls a “liberty of indifference” — the idea that free action requires actions to be uncaused or to arise without motives. He rejects this as unintelligible and contrary to experience. If actions were detached from motives and character, they would be random, not responsible. Such randomness would not improve freedom; it would undermine it.

For Hume, moral responsibility depends on the fact that actions express stable features of the person. We praise, blame, reward, and punish because actions are connected to motives and character in a regular way. If conduct were not causally linked to these features, moral practices would lose their basis. Thus necessity is not an enemy of responsibility; it is what makes responsibility intelligible.

A key part of his argument is that the same principles that explain why we hold people accountable also explain why we distinguish voluntary from involuntary action. We excuse actions caused by force, ignorance, or lack of control, but hold persons responsible when actions flow from their will. This shows that freedom is a matter of acting through one’s own volition, not of being uncaused.

In short, Hume’s compatibilism claims:
1. Human actions are causally regular and thus “necessary” in the relevant sense.
2. Liberty consists in acting according to one’s will without external restraint.
3. Therefore, liberty and necessity are compatible.
4. Moral responsibility depends on this compatibility, since social and ethical judgment require causal regularity in human behavior.
