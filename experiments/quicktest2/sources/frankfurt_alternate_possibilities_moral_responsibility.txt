RATIONALE: Seminal paper for the claim that alternative possibilities are not required for responsibility, central to debates over the conditional "could have done otherwise" and Frankfurt-style cases.

========================================================================

Frankfurt’s central claim is that a person can be morally responsible for what they do even if they could not have done otherwise. This challenges the traditional idea that responsibility requires genuine alternative possibilities.

The paper argues against the “Principle of Alternate Possibilities” (often abbreviated PAP): the view that a person is morally responsible for an action only if they could have done something else. Frankfurt thinks this principle is false, or at least not necessary for explaining responsibility.

His strategy is to imagine cases in which an agent appears fully responsible for an action, but where an intervening device or external controller stands ready to ensure that the agent would perform the same action no matter what. In the classic structure of these cases, the agent deliberates and acts on their own, and the controlling mechanism never needs to intervene because the agent independently does exactly what the controller wants. In that situation, the agent seems responsible for the action, even though alternative possibilities were effectively unavailable. This is meant to show that the absence of alternatives does not automatically remove responsibility.

A key point in Frankfurt’s argument is that what matters for responsibility is not the mere availability of other options, but the actual source of the action—whether the action issues from the agent’s own motives, choices, or will. If the agent acts from their own reasons and decides on their own, responsibility can remain intact even if hidden constraints make it impossible for them to do otherwise.

Frankfurt also distinguishes between two questions: whether an action was voluntary or up to the agent, and whether the agent had open alternatives. He argues that moral appraisal should focus on the relation between the person and the actual action, not on hypothetical possibilities that never become relevant.

The paper became famous because it shifts the debate about moral responsibility away from the simple conditional “could have done otherwise” and toward a more refined account of agency, control, and the actual causal role of the agent’s will. It also helped launch the literature on “Frankfurt-style cases,” which are designed to test whether responsibility depends on alternative possibilities at all.
