Strawson’s central claim is that ordinary moral responsibility does not rest primarily on a metaphysical proof that human beings are uncaused or undetermined. Instead, it rests on the everyday interpersonal attitudes we take toward one another in relationships. These attitudes are what he calls the "reactive attitudes."

His starting point is that human life normally involves participation in a network of expectations, demands, and responses. When others treat us with goodwill, disregard, or harm, we naturally respond with feelings such as resentment, gratitude, forgiveness, hurt, indignation, guilt, and love. These responses are not philosophical add-ons; they are built into ordinary human relationships. Praise and blame are linked to these attitudes because they express, reinforce, or modify them.

Strawson argues that questions about whether determinism is true do not undermine these practices in the way some philosophers fear. Even if determinism were true, it would not follow that resentment, gratitude, blame, or praise become irrational. These reactions are part of the human "participant" standpoint from which we live with and regard one another as agents. The deeper issue is not whether actions are metaphysically free in some ultimate sense, but whether a person’s conduct shows the kinds of qualities—such as ill will, indifference, or good will—that make these attitudes appropriate.

He distinguishes the "participant attitude" from the "objective attitude." In the participant attitude, we regard others as responsible agents and respond to them directly with the reactive attitudes. In the objective attitude, we may come to see someone more as an object of management, treatment, training, or control rather than as a full participant in reciprocal moral relations. This shift can be appropriate in cases involving children, severe mental illness, coercion, or deep impairment. But Strawson thinks a world in which we universally adopted the objective attitude would be a radically altered and less human world.

A major part of the essay is the idea that responsibility practices are grounded in interpersonal human life, not in abstract metaphysical theories. We sometimes excuse or exempt people from blame because of accident, incapacity, ignorance, compulsion, or serious abnormality. Those exemptions show that our reactive attitudes are sensitive to understanding, not that they depend on indeterminism. Moral responsibility is therefore tied to whether the person stands in the right relation to the action and the injured party, not to whether the action could have been otherwise in a libertarian sense.

Strawson’s broader conclusion is that debates about determinism and freedom often miss the basis of moral life. The practices of praise and blame are sustained by our human forms of relationship, and they will remain justified so long as we continue to live as persons who care about one another’s intentions, goodwill, and regard. Determinism, even if true, would not by itself eliminate the legitimacy of these practices.
