========================================================================

Frankfurt’s central claim is that moral responsibility does not require genuine alternatives. He challenges the Principle of Alternate Possibilities (PAP), the idea that a person is morally responsible for what they do only if they could have done otherwise.

To make this point, he uses a hypothetical case in which an agent is left free to choose and act on their own, but a hidden intervener is ready to force the outcome if the agent seems likely to choose differently. In the version of the case Frankfurt discusses, the intervener never actually has to interfere, because the agent chooses the act the intervener wanted anyway. The important point is that the agent could not have done otherwise in the relevant sense, since any contrary attempt would have been blocked, yet the agent still appears morally responsible because the actual decision was made from the agent’s own will, without intervention.

Frankfurt’s argument is meant to show that the existence of open alternatives is not what makes an act morally attributable to a person. What matters more is whether the action issues from the agent’s own motives, intentions, and decision-making, rather than from external compulsion or manipulation. He therefore concludes that PAP is false: the lack of alternatives does not by itself eliminate moral responsibility.

The paper became famous because it shifted the debate about free will and responsibility away from the simple question “Could the person have done otherwise?” and toward whether responsibility can survive in cases where the actual action is fully owned by the agent even though alternatives were unavailable.
