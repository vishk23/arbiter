========================================================================

Ayer’s central claim is that the traditional conflict between freedom and determinism is often misstated. He argues that a person can be genuinely free and morally responsible even if every action has a cause. What matters for freedom is not exemption from causation, but whether the action issues from the agent’s own motives, desires, and character rather than from coercion, compulsion, or external restraint.

Ayer distinguishes two different senses of “can” or “could have done otherwise.” In one sense, a person could have acted differently if their preferences or reasons had been different; in this sense, freedom is compatible with causal determination. In the stronger sense, an action would be free only if it were uncaused or not determined by prior conditions; Ayer rejects this as unnecessary for responsibility. He treats the demand for an uncaused choice as a mistake that confuses freedom with randomness.

His compatibilist view is that a person acts freely when they do what they want to do, are not physically forced, and are not acting under coercion or pathological compulsion. If an action expresses the agent’s settled motives and character, then it can still be free even if those motives themselves have causal antecedents. On this account, determinism does not erase the difference between voluntary action and action produced by violence, threats, or irresistible constraint.

Ayer also connects this to moral responsibility. Praise, blame, reward, and punishment remain intelligible because they respond to conduct that flows from the agent’s dispositions and can be influenced by reasons, incentives, and sanctions. Thus, even in a causally ordered world, responsibility has a practical role: it helps shape future conduct and reflects the fact that the action came from the person rather than from external compulsion.

Overall, the essay is a classic defense of compatibilism: freedom is not the absence of causes, but the absence of the right kind of interference.
