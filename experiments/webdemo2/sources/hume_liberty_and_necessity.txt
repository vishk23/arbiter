Hume’s central claim in this section is that liberty and necessity are compatible. He argues that the debate is often confused because each side uses the key terms in a different sense.

Hume first defines **liberty** in a practical, common-sense way: a person is free when they can act or refrain from acting according to the determination of their will, without external constraint or force. So liberty is not the power to will without causes; it is the ability to carry out what one wants, if nothing prevents it.

He then defines **necessity** not as metaphysical compulsion, but as the regular connection we observe between causes and effects. In human life, necessity means that similar motives, characters, and circumstances tend to produce similar actions, and that from observing one we can infer the other. Hume says this is the same kind of necessity we rely on everywhere in reasoning about the world.

A major part of his argument is that **human behavior is governed by stable patterns**. We infer people’s actions from their motives, character, and situation just as we infer physical events from their causes. History, politics, law, and ordinary social life all depend on this regularity. If human actions were not causally connected to motives and character, we could not predict behavior, assign blame, reward virtue, or make sense of moral evaluation.

Hume also argues that **moral responsibility requires necessity** in this sense. We praise and blame people because their actions express their character and motives. If actions were completely disconnected from causes, they would be random rather than morally assessable. So far from undermining responsibility, necessity is what makes responsibility intelligible.

The apparent conflict disappears once the terms are properly understood: **liberty is freedom from external constraint**, while **necessity is the regular causal connection between motives and actions**. A person can be free in Hume’s sense even if their choices have causes. Thus, free action and causal determination are compatible.

Finally, Hume suggests that much of the controversy is verbal. People often oppose liberty to necessity by imagining that freedom must mean the absence of all causation. Hume rejects that assumption and concludes that, in ordinary human affairs, liberty and necessity are not enemies but complementary ideas.
