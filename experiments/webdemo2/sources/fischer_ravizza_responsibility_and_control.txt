Fischer and Ravizza argue for a compatibilist theory of moral responsibility: a person can be morally responsible even if determinism is true, so long as the person has the right kind of control over action. Their central claim is that responsibility does not require the power to do otherwise in a libertarian sense; instead, it requires a kind of *guidance control* rooted in reasons-responsive mechanisms.

The book’s basic strategy is to separate two issues that are often run together: (1) whether an agent’s action is produced by the right kind of causal process, and (2) whether the agent has the metaphysical freedom to choose among genuinely open alternatives. Fischer and Ravizza argue that moral responsibility depends on the first issue, not the second. What matters is not an indeterministic “forking” ability, but whether the actual mechanism leading to action is sufficiently receptive to reasons and appropriately owned by the agent.

A central concept is *reasons-responsiveness*. An action is controlled in the relevant sense when it issues from a mechanism that can recognize and react to reasons. Fischer and Ravizza distinguish between moderate and strong forms of this capacity: responsibility does not require that an agent respond to every possible reason, but it does require that the mechanism would operate differently in a range of nearby possible circumstances if sufficient reasons were present. This makes responsibility compatible with causal determinism, since a deterministic mechanism can still be reasons-responsive.

They also emphasize *guidance control* versus *regulative control*. Regulative control is the stronger idea: the ability to choose among alternatives in a way that is not fixed by prior causes. Guidance control is weaker but sufficient for moral responsibility: the action is guided by the agent’s own reasons-responsive mechanism, even if the overall causal story is determined. On their view, moral responsibility attaches to the quality of the actual mechanism, not to an unrealized power to have done otherwise.

Another major element is *ownership* of the mechanism. It is not enough that a reasons-responsive mechanism causes the action; the mechanism must be “owned” by the agent. Ownership is built through a process of taking responsibility, in which an individual comes to see herself as an apt participant in the moral community—someone whose actions can be evaluated and who can be held accountable. This historical and developmental condition helps explain why some actions produced by pathological or externally implanted mechanisms may not be attributable to the agent in the full responsibility-bearing sense.

The theory therefore combines a structural condition and a historical condition. Structurally, the mechanism must be reasons-responsive; historically, the agent must have the right kind of relation to that mechanism, such that it counts as hers. Together these conditions aim to explain why a person can be responsible for ordinary intentional actions but not for behavior produced by manipulation, coercion, or deviant causal histories that bypass genuine agency.

Fischer and Ravizza also argue that responsibility is compatible with determinism because what matters is not indeterministic openness but actual ownership and the right sort of causal control. A determined action can still be the product of the agent’s own deliberative capacities, and thus attributable to her. They reject the claim that moral responsibility requires ultimate origination in a way that is independent of prior causal history.

Overall, the book’s importance lies in offering a major compatibilist framework that explains moral responsibility in terms of control, reasons-responsiveness, and ownership, rather than metaphysical indeterminism. It is one of the most influential contemporary attempts to ground responsibility in an agent’s actual deliberative capacities while preserving the compatibility of responsibility with determinism.
