========================================================================

Harry Frankfurt’s paper is a landmark critique of the principle that a person is morally responsible only if they could have done otherwise. The central target is the Principle of Alternate Possibilities (PAP), the idea that moral responsibility requires access to at least one genuine alternative course of action.

Frankfurt argues that PAP is false by describing cases in which a person seems morally responsible even though no genuine alternative was available. His famous strategy is to construct cases where an agent performs an action on his own, but a hidden intervener would have forced the same action if the agent had been about to choose otherwise. Because the intervener never needs to act, the agent’s actual decision remains fully his own, yet there was never a real alternative. Frankfurt uses this to suggest that the mere absence of alternatives does not by itself remove responsibility.

The paper’s key structure is:
1. Identify PAP as a widespread assumption in debates about free will and responsibility.
2. Show that ordinary intuitions about responsibility can survive even when alternatives are removed in advance.
3. Conclude that moral responsibility depends on something other than the availability of alternative possibilities.

Frankfurt’s argument relies on the idea that what matters for responsibility is not simply whether the agent could have done otherwise, but whether the action issued from the agent’s own will, motives, or decisions in the actual sequence of events. In his view, an agent may be responsible if the action is genuinely his, even when all alternative possibilities are blocked.

A major implication is that compatibilist theories of freedom gain support: if responsibility does not require alternatives, then determinism is not automatically incompatible with moral responsibility. Frankfurt does not claim to have fully explained responsibility, but he aims to undermine a common necessity claim that had been taken as central.

The paper also introduces a major methodological lesson for the free will debate: thought experiments can separate the intuitive pull of responsibility from the assumption that alternatives are necessary. Later discussion of “Frankfurt cases” often centers on whether the hidden intervener genuinely eliminates alternatives in the right way, but Frankfurt’s original point is that alternate possibilities are not obviously required for responsibility.

In short, the paper’s enduring contribution is the claim that moral responsibility is compatible with a lack of alternate possibilities, and that responsibility should instead be grounded in features of the agent’s actual choice and control.
