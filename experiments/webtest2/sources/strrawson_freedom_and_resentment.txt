RATIONALE: Canonical essay on moral responsibility, reactive attitudes, and the compatibilist debate about agency and blame.

========================================================================

Strawson’s central claim is that debates about whether determinism is true do not by themselves settle the question of moral responsibility, because our ordinary practices of praise, blame, resentment, gratitude, forgiveness, and similar responses are grounded in human interpersonal life rather than in a theoretical proof of metaphysical freedom.

The essay introduces the idea of the “reactive attitudes.” These are the ordinary emotional and interpersonal responses we have toward others when we treat one another as participants in a moral community. Examples include resentment when we are wronged, gratitude when we are benefited, indignation on behalf of a victim, hurt feelings, forgiveness, and love. Strawson argues that these attitudes are not optional add-ons to morality; they are basic features of how we relate to persons as responsible agents.

He contrasts the “participant attitude” with the “objective attitude.” The participant attitude treats people as engaged members of a shared social world, where expectations, demands, and interpersonal accountability naturally arise. The objective attitude, by contrast, is a more detached stance in which we explain, manage, or treat someone as an object of policy, therapy, or control rather than as a fully responsible participant. Strawson does not say the objective attitude is never appropriate; rather, it is appropriate in special cases such as immaturity, severe mental illness, coercion, or profound incapacity.

A major point of the essay is that moral responsibility is tied to this network of interpersonal attitudes. To resent someone is not merely to register that they caused harm; it is to express the view that they have violated a legitimate expectation rooted in human relations. Likewise, to hold someone responsible is to keep them within the practice of mutual accountability. Strawson suggests that even if determinism were true, it would not automatically force us to abandon these attitudes, because they are constitutive of human relationships and social life.

He also distinguishes between ordinary cases and exceptional cases where we excuse or exempt someone. Excuses and exemptions are not based only on abstract metaphysics; they often depend on whether the person’s action showed a relevant ill will, disregard, or capacity for participation in mutual expectations. When someone acts under duress, ignorance, accident, or certain forms of impairment, we may suspend resentment because the connection between the action and the agent’s quality of will is weakened.

Strawson’s broader compatibilist-style conclusion is that the demand for a theoretical guarantee of absolute freedom is misplaced if what we are asking about is whether moral responsibility can survive determinism. Since our practices of blame and praise are embedded in our forms of life, they do not depend on proving that human action is uncaused in a metaphysical sense. The real issue is whether the conditions for appropriate reactive attitudes are present.

He also suggests that fully abandoning reactive attitudes in favor of a purely detached, objective stance would be psychologically impossible or at least deeply alienating for ordinary human life. Such a shift would amount to leaving behind interpersonal moral relationships themselves, not merely revising a theory. Thus the essay reframes the compatibilism debate: instead of asking whether determinism is compatible with some abstract freedom, we should ask what attitudes and practices are actually constitutive of responsibility among persons.
