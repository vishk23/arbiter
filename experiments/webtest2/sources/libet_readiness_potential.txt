Libet’s 1983 work is a classic study of the temporal relation between brain activity and the conscious experience of deciding to act. The central question is whether a person becomes consciously aware of the intention to move before the brain has already begun preparing that movement, or whether conscious intention comes first and triggers the brain process.

The study builds on the idea of the readiness potential (RP), a slow buildup of electrical activity in the brain that appears before self-initiated voluntary movement. Libet asked subjects to make simple, spontaneous actions, such as flexing a wrist or finger, whenever they wished. While they did this, EEG was used to detect the readiness potential, EMG marked the actual movement, and subjects reported the moment they first became aware of the intention or urge to move by using a fast-moving clock-like reference.

The main finding was that the readiness potential began well before the reported time of conscious intention. In the classic pattern reported by Libet, brain activity associated with preparing the act started roughly half a second before movement, while the conscious experience of deciding to act was reported only a couple of hundred milliseconds before movement. This temporal order suggested that the neural preparation for a voluntary act begins unconsciously and that conscious intention appears later.

Libet did not present this as a simple refutation of free will. Instead, he argued that conscious will may not initiate the action in the first place, but it may still play a supervisory role. In particular, he proposed the possibility of a conscious “veto” or “free won’t”: even if the brain begins preparing an action unconsciously, conscious awareness might still be able to stop it before execution.

The broader significance of the paper is that it became a central reference in debates about whether neuroscience undermines free will or merely describes the mechanisms by which actions are prepared and carried out. The study is often cited to support the claim that conscious decision-making may lag behind neural initiation, but it is also widely discussed because the task was artificial, the timing report method has limitations, and the readiness potential may reflect a general preparation or bias to act rather than a fixed decision itself.
