RATIONALE: Foundational essay on reactive attitudes and responsibility, central to compatibilist accounts that emphasize agency and responsibility without metaphysical indeterminism.

========================================================================

Strawson’s central claim is that our practices of holding people responsible are grounded less in a metaphysical theory of free will than in ordinary interpersonal relationships. When someone treats us well or badly, we naturally respond with "reactive attitudes" such as resentment, gratitude, forgiveness, indignation, hurt, and love. These attitudes express our expectations about one another’s quality of will, concern, and regard.

He argues that these reactive attitudes are not optional extras added to morality from outside; they are part of the basic human framework of social life. Because we are participants in relationships, we ordinarily respond to one another as responsible agents. The demand for moral accountability belongs to this interpersonal stance. Strawson contrasts this with the "objective attitude," in which we suspend these reactions and instead treat a person as an object of management, treatment, or explanation rather than as a moral agent.

A key part of the essay is the claim that philosophical debates about determinism do not undermine these practices in the way some have hoped or feared. Even if determinism were true, Strawson thinks we would still continue to rely on reactive attitudes, because they are embedded in human life and communication. The real question is not whether determinism is metaphysically compatible with responsibility in an abstract sense, but whether we can intelligibly give up the interpersonal attitudes that constitute responsibility practices.

Strawson also notes that there are cases where the full reactive framework is suspended or weakened: for example, with young children, the mentally ill, or people judged to be acting under severe constraint, ignorance, or incapacity. In such cases, we may shift toward the objective attitude and explain behavior in causal or clinical terms. But these are exceptions that help reveal the normal rule: moral responsibility depends on the capacity to participate in interpersonal moral life.

The essay is therefore often read as a compatibilist classic, but its distinctive contribution is not a technical proof that free will and determinism are logically consistent. Instead, Strawson reframes the issue: responsibility is rooted in the structure of human relationships, in our emotional and normative responses to one another, and in our need to regard others as fellow participants in the moral community.
