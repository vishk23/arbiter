Dennett’s central claim is that the kind of free will people actually need for agency, morality, and responsibility does not require a metaphysically open universe. He rejects the idea that freedom must involve being uncaused or exempt from determinism. Instead, he argues that what matters is whether an agent has enough "elbow room" to deliberate, anticipate consequences, learn from experience, and adjust behavior in light of reasons.

A major part of the book is a critique of the false choice between determinism and freedom. Dennett says determinism does not imply fatalism: even if events have causes, our reasoning, planning, and self-control are themselves causal processes that make a difference. Randomness would not help, either, because merely unpredictable choices would not amount to responsible agency. The freedom worth wanting is therefore not indeterministic spontaneity, but reliable control that is sensitive to information and reasons.

Dennett also ties free will to evolution and the development of increasingly sophisticated organisms. Biological evolution gradually creates systems that can model their environments, foresee dangers, and choose among options. Human beings have especially rich capacities for reflection and self-regulation, which expand the range of futures they can influence. In that sense, freedom is not the absence of causation but a highly evolved form of causal competence.

On responsibility, Dennett argues that praise, blame, coercion, manipulation, addiction, and compulsion are meaningful because they track differences in an agent’s responsiveness to reasons and ability to control conduct. A person is more responsible when they can understand norms, consider alternatives, and respond to incentives or arguments; responsibility is reduced when those capacities are undermined. He therefore defends moral practices that distinguish ordinary agency from coercion or alien control.

Overall, Elbow Room is a compatibilist defense of human agency: we can have meaningful freedom, deliberation, and responsibility in a deterministic world because freedom is a matter of practical control, not supernatural exception from causal order.
