========================================================================

Frankfurt’s central target is the Principle of Alternate Possibilities (PAP): the idea that a person is morally responsible for what they do only if they could have done otherwise. He argues that this principle is false, and that moral responsibility can remain even when no genuine alternative course of action was available.

The paper’s core strategy is to construct cases in which an agent appears fully responsible for an action, yet could not have done otherwise because some other factor would have ensured the same action if the agent had begun to choose differently. In the classic structure of these cases, an external intervener stands ready to force the outcome if the agent were about to choose a different option, but does not actually intervene because the agent independently makes the “right” choice on their own. In that situation, the agent’s actual action is voluntary and attributable to their own will, even though alternate possibilities were never really open.

From this, Frankfurt concludes that the absence of alternatives does not by itself remove responsibility. What matters morally is not merely whether the agent had other options, but whether the action issued from the agent’s own will, intentions, and reasons in the actual sequence of events. If the person acted on their own and was not coerced or overridden in the relevant way, responsibility can still be present even in a deterministic or otherwise fixed setting.

The paper therefore shifts attention away from a simple “could have done otherwise” test toward the actual source of action. It challenges a long-standing assumption in ethics and philosophy of action: that freedom and responsibility require open alternatives. Frankfurt’s argument became famous because it offered a direct compatibilist challenge to PAP and suggested that moral responsibility depends more on actual control and ownership of action than on live possibilities that never get used.
