========================================================================

Fischer and Ravizza’s book is a landmark compatibilist account of moral responsibility. Its central claim is that moral responsibility does not require the power to do otherwise; instead, what matters is a kind of control they call **guidance control**. Guidance control is distinct from **regulative control**, which is the ability to choose among genuinely open alternative possibilities. The authors argue that even in a deterministic world, agents can still be morally responsible if their actions issue from the right kind of mechanism.

Their account turns on two main conditions. First, the mechanism that produces the action must be **moderately reasons-responsive**: roughly, it must be sensitive to reasons in a way that would lead the agent to act differently in some nearby possible situations where there were sufficiently strong reasons to do so. This is weaker than requiring full-blown libertarian openness, but stronger than merely acting from a pattern or habit. Second, the mechanism must be **owned** by the agent. Ownership is a historical condition: the mechanism has to become the agent’s own through a process in which the person comes to take responsibility for it, understands herself as an originator of action, and sees herself as appropriately answerable to reasons, praise, and blame.

This historical “taking responsibility” condition is important for handling manipulation cases. A person may be perfectly reasons-responsive in the present, yet still not be morally responsible if the mechanism was implanted or controlled in a way that bypassed genuine ownership. Fischer and Ravizza therefore try to explain why ordinary agents are responsible while manipulated agents are not, without appealing to indeterminism.

The book also defends **semi-compatibilism**, the view that even if free will in a stronger sense turned out to be incompatible with determinism, **moral responsibility** still is not. So the authors separate the issue of responsible agency from the issue of whether alternatives are metaphysically open. They also use **tracing** to explain cases where someone is responsible for an action even if the action itself is not directly under guidance control, because responsibility can be traced back to earlier culpable choices that led to the present action.

Overall, the book’s major contribution is a naturalistic, compatibilist theory of responsibility centered on actual-sequence control, reasons-responsiveness, and the historical ownership of one’s action-guiding mechanisms.
