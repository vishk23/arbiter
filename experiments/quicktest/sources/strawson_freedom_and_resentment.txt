RATIONALE: Foundational paper on moral responsibility, reactive attitudes, and why responsibility may not depend on metaphysical alternative possibilities.

========================================================================

Strawson’s central claim is that our practices of moral responsibility are grounded less in abstract metaphysics than in the ordinary human relationships expressed through what he calls the "reactive attitudes." These include resentment, gratitude, forgiveness, hurt feelings, anger, love, and indignation. We naturally adopt these attitudes toward one another because we see each other as participants in interpersonal life—beings whose actions matter to us in ways that invite praise, blame, apology, resentment, or gratitude.

From this starting point, Strawson argues that the traditional debate over whether determinism is true does not, by itself, undermine moral responsibility. Even if determinism were true, human beings would still live in relationships structured by these attitudes. Responsibility does not depend primarily on having metaphysically “free” choices in the sense of being outside causal order. Instead, it depends on whether agents are the proper targets of the reactive attitudes, given their intentions, character, and treatment of others.

A major part of the essay distinguishes between two kinds of cases in which reactive attitudes are modified or suspended. First are excusing conditions, where someone’s action does not warrant resentment because of factors like accident, coercion, ignorance, or lack of intention. Second are exempting conditions, where we suspend ordinary expectations because the person is not fully able to participate in moral relationships—for example, small children, the severely mentally ill, or those whose capacities are seriously impaired [UNCERTAIN: this list is representative rather than exhaustive]. In such cases, we may adopt what Strawson calls the "objective attitude," treating the person more like an object of policy, prediction, training, or management than as a full partner in moral interaction.

Strawson’s important philosophical move is to say that the objective attitude is real but limited. It can explain how we sometimes step back from ordinary interpersonal exchange, but it cannot replace the participant stance that makes moral life possible. A world in which we permanently abandoned reactive attitudes would be a radically different kind of human world—one stripped of the relationships that give moral responsibility its meaning.

He also suggests that the debate between determinism and indeterminism is partly misguided because it asks whether a metaphysical thesis would justify our practices from a detached standpoint. Strawson thinks this detached standpoint is not one we can fully occupy, since our moral life is embedded in human community. The real question is not whether responsibility can be proven from outside human practices, but how those practices function and when they are appropriately suspended.

Overall, the paper is foundational because it shifts attention away from abstract free-will theories and toward the interpersonal basis of responsibility. Its lasting influence comes from the idea that moral accountability is rooted in the attitudes and expectations constitutive of human social life, not in a requirement that agents be uncaused or able to do otherwise in some absolute metaphysical sense.
