Hume argues that the apparent conflict between liberty and necessity is mostly verbal: once the terms are properly defined, human freedom and causal necessity are compatible. He begins by rejecting the idea that necessity means a mysterious power in causes. For Hume, necessity in nature is nothing more than the regular conjunction of events plus the mind’s habit of inferring one event from another. We call an event necessary when similar causes have been constantly followed by similar effects, so that we confidently expect the effect from the cause.

Applied to human behavior, Hume claims that actions exhibit the same sort of regularity as natural events. People’s conduct shows stable patterns across circumstances, characters, motives, and social conditions. Because of this, we can make reliable predictions about behavior, form judgments of character, and explain actions by reference to motives and dispositions. This regularity is evidence that human actions are part of the causal order.

Hume then defines liberty in a way that preserves moral responsibility: liberty is not the absence of causation, but the power to act or not act according to the will, without external constraint or coercion. If I act from my own choice, and not because I am physically forced or externally compelled, I am free—even if my choice itself has causes. Thus liberty and necessity are compatible, because necessity concerns the causal determination of actions, while liberty concerns the absence of restraint in carrying out the will.

He also argues that moral practices depend on this kind of necessity. Praise, blame, punishment, reward, and legal accountability all presuppose that actions flow from stable motives and character. If human actions were entirely uncaused or random, then character could not be inferred from conduct, and moral appraisal would lose its foundation. So necessity is not a threat to morality; it is what makes moral evaluation possible.

Finally, Hume contends that disputes about free will often arise from equivocation. If liberty is taken to mean indifference or uncaused choice, then it conflicts with necessity; but if liberty means acting according to one’s will without external compulsion, it is fully consistent with causal determination. On this view, the controversy is largely resolved by careful definition rather than by deep metaphysical opposition.
