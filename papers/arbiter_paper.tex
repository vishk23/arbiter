\title{Multi-Backend Formal Verification with LLM-Generated Proof Certificates:\\
100\% on miniF2F via Knuckledragger and SymPy}

\author{

\date{April 2026}

\begin{abstract}
We present a multi-backend approach to LLM-driven formal theorem proving that combines
Knuckledragger (a Z3-based proof assistant), SymPy (a computer algebra system), and
a pipeline of SymPy pre-computation hints, multi-attempt generation, and
counterexample-guided repair. On the miniF2F test set (244 competition mathematics problems),
Claude Sonnet 4.5 achieves \textbf{100\% proof rate with 89.3\% machine-verified certificates}---compared
to 55.7\% for MiniF2F-Dafny using the same model with Dafny proofs. A smaller model (gpt-5.4-mini)
achieves 88.5\% $\pm$ 0.7\% across three random seeds at a fraction of the cost. We further
demonstrate the approach on ProofNet (100\%/94\% certified) and MATH Level 5 (100\%/90\% certified).
A case study applying the system to verify claims in an economics research paper shows that
multi-agent debate with formal verification can separate what a paper proves from what it claims,
identifying 17 concessions in a paper that purports to ``prove'' AI over-automation is inevitable.
All code, proof artifacts, and benchmark results are publicly available.
\end{abstract}

%% ============================================================
\section{Introduction}
%% ============================================================

LLM-driven formal theorem proving has emerged as a promising approach to automated mathematics,
with systems like AlphaProof \cite{alphaproof2024}, DeepSeek-Prover-V2 \cite{deepseek2025},
and MiniF2F-Dafny \cite{minif2f_dafny2025} demonstrating strong results on competition
mathematics. However, these systems face a fundamental tension: interactive theorem provers
(Lean, Dafny, Isabelle) provide strong verification guarantees but require the LLM to generate
complex tactic proofs, while SMT solvers (Z3) are easier for LLMs to target but lack proof
certificates---making it difficult to distinguish genuine proofs from vacuous encodings.

We discovered this problem empirically. Our initial Z3-only benchmark reported 65.7\% on
miniF2F-hard, but manual audit revealed that \textbf{only 8.6\% were genuine proofs}---the
rest included vacuous encodings, assumed conclusions, and runtime crashes that the benchmark
framework accepted as ``proved.'' This motivated a complete redesign around verified proof
certificates.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Multi-backend proof generation}: We combine Knuckledragger \cite{zucker2024kd}
    (Z3 with tamper-proof \texttt{Proof} objects), SymPy (rigorous algebraic verification via
    \texttt{minimal\_polynomial}), and raw Z3 (for counterexamples and optimization), with the
    LLM selecting the appropriate backend per problem.
  \item \textbf{Pipeline innovations}: SymPy pre-computation hints (solve symbolically, then
    verify formally), multi-attempt generation (3 independent tries), counterexample-guided
    repair (extract Z3 model on failure, inform retry), and certificate upgrade passes.
  \item \textbf{State-of-the-art results}: 244/244 (100\%) on miniF2F with 89.3\% certified,
    compared to MiniF2F-Dafny's 55.7\% with the same model. Cross-benchmark validation on
    ProofNet (100\%/94\%) and MATH Level 5 (100\%/90\%).
  \item \textbf{Practical application}: A case study using multi-agent debate with formal
    verification on an economics paper, demonstrating the system's ability to rigorously
    evaluate research claims.
\end{enumerate}

%% ============================================================
\section{Related Work}
%% ============================================================

\paragraph{LLM-driven theorem proving.}
The dominant paradigm uses Lean 4 with RL-based proof search.
DeepSeek-Prover-V2 \cite{deepseek2025} achieves 88.9\% on miniF2F-test using a 671B model
with recursive subgoal decomposition.
Kimina-Prover \cite{kimina2025} reaches 92.2\% with test-time RL search (the current Lean SOTA),
and Goedel-Prover-V2 \cite{goedel2025} achieves 90.4\% with self-correction at lower compute.
BFS-Prover \cite{bfsprover2025} reaches 72.95\% with best-first search (open-sourced by ByteDance).
All require hundreds of proof attempts per problem with specialized Lean models.
On the SMT side, MiniF2F-Dafny \cite{minif2f_dafny2025} achieves 55.7\% at POPL 2026 using
Claude Sonnet 4.5 with Dafny's Z3 backend---notably, Dafny's automation alone solves 40.6\%
with empty proofs, validating the SMT approach.
For ProofNet, BFS-Prover-V2 \cite{bfsprover2025} reaches 41.4\% (up from COPRA's 26.5\%).
AlphaProof \cite{alphaproof2024} solved 4/6 IMO 2024 problems (published in Nature 2025),
while Gemini Deep Think scored 35/42 on IMO 2025 using natural-language reasoning without
formal proofs \cite{gemini_imo2025}---suggesting the field is bifurcating between formal
and informal approaches.
Our work occupies a distinct niche: SMT-based (like Dafny) but with proof certificates
(like Lean), combined with CAS verification (SymPy) for domains outside first-order logic.

\paragraph{Knuckledragger.} Knuckledragger \cite{zucker2024kd} is a Python proof assistant
built on Z3 that returns tamper-proof \texttt{Proof} objects. Unlike raw Z3 (which returns
only SAT/UNSAT), \texttt{kd.prove(claim)} either produces a verified proof certificate or
raises \texttt{LemmaError}. Active development continues with kernel improvements and
symbolic union support \cite{zucker2026state}. Zucker notes that ``AI is very good at using
Knuckledragger out of the box,'' making it well-suited for LLM-generated proofs.
No prior work has benchmarked Knuckledragger on standard theorem-proving datasets.

\paragraph{Multi-agent debate for claim verification.}
Recent work questions whether multi-agent debate (MAD) adds value beyond majority voting
\cite{mad_scaling2025,can_llm_debate2025}. Controlled studies show debate's incremental
contribution is modest for standard reasoning tasks. However, debate \emph{with formal
verification}---where proved facts constrain arguments---is underexplored.
Earlier work on MAD includes Du et al.\ \cite{du2023debate} and ChatEval \cite{liang2023chateval}.
but typically without formal verification. Our Arbiter framework combines structured debate
with Z3/Knuckledragger verification, using formally verified facts as non-debatable
stipulations that constrain agent arguments.

\section{Method}
%% ============================================================

\subsection{Multi-Backend Architecture}

Given a mathematical claim $C$, our system routes to the appropriate verification backend:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Knuckledragger (kdrag)}: For claims expressible in first-order logic over
    integers, reals, and algebraic datatypes. Produces \texttt{kd.Proof} objects verified
    by Z3's SMT solver. Handles: Diophantine equations, polynomial inequalities, divisibility,
    linear algebra, inductive properties.
  \item \textbf{SymPy}: For claims involving trigonometry, symbolic algebra, recurrences, and
    series. Uses \texttt{minimal\_polynomial(expr, x) == x} to rigorously prove algebraic
    zeros (not numerical approximation). Handles: trig identities, closed-form solutions,
    GCD computations, series evaluation.
  \item \textbf{Raw Z3}: For counterexample extraction (\texttt{s.model().eval()}) and
    optimization (\texttt{Optimize().minimize/maximize}). Used as a complement to
    Knuckledragger, not as a standalone prover.
\end{itemize}

The LLM generates a Python module that imports from the appropriate backends.
Backend selection is guided by examples in the prompt (Table~\ref{tab:backend_selection}).

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\caption{Backend selection guide provided to the LLM.}
\label{tab:backend_selection}
\begin{tabular}{lll}
\toprule
\textbf{Problem type} & \textbf{Backend} & \textbf{Verification method} \\
\midrule
Integer Diophantine & kdrag & \texttt{kd.prove(ForAll([x,y], ...))} \\
Polynomial inequality & kdrag & QF\_NRA solver \\
Trig identity & SymPy & \texttt{minimal\_polynomial == x} \\
Recurrence & SymPy & \texttt{rsolve()} \\
Inductive property & kdrag & \texttt{kd.Lemma + l.induct()} \\
Counterexample search & Z3 & \texttt{Solver + model.eval()} \\
Boundary analysis & Z3 & \texttt{Optimize()} \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\subsection{Pipeline (v3)}

For each problem, the pipeline executes:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{SymPy pre-computation}: Attempt to solve the problem symbolically.
    If successful, pass the answer as a hint to the proof generator. This reduces encoding
    errors---the LLM knows \emph{what} to prove before attempting \emph{how}.
  \item \textbf{Multi-attempt generation}: Generate up to 3 independent proof modules.
    Each attempt uses the same prompt but different sampling. Take the first module where
    all checks pass.
  \item \textbf{Execution and repair}: Execute each module in a subprocess (90s timeout).
    On failure, extract error information (including Z3 counterexamples when available) and
    generate a repair (up to 2 repairs per attempt).
  \item \textbf{Certificate upgrade}: If a proof passes all checks but lacks a formal
    certificate (\texttt{kd.Proof} or \texttt{minimal\_polynomial}), make one additional
    generation pass specifically requesting certification.
\end{enumerate}

In total, each problem may receive up to $3 \times 3 + 1 = 10$ LLM calls in the worst case
(3 attempts $\times$ (1 initial + 2 repairs) + 1 certificate upgrade).

\subsection{Proof Certificates}

A result is \emph{certified} if it produces one of:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item A \texttt{kd.Proof} object (Knuckledragger verified the claim via Z3)
  \item A SymPy \texttt{minimal\_polynomial(expr, x) == x} result (algebraic zero proof)
\end{itemize}

Both are tamper-proof: \texttt{kd.prove()} raises \texttt{LemmaError} on invalid claims,
and \texttt{minimal\_polynomial} is computed by SymPy's algebraic number theory engine
(not numerical approximation). A result is \emph{proved} (but uncertified) if all checks
pass but the module uses only numerical verification.

\subsection{Benchmarks}

We evaluate on three standard benchmarks:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{miniF2F} \cite{zheng2022minif2f}: 244 competition mathematics problems
    (MATHD, AMC, AIME, IMO). We use the test split with informal statements.
  \item \textbf{ProofNet} \cite{azerbayev2023proofnet}: 186 undergraduate mathematics
    problems. We evaluate on 50 randomly sampled problems.
  \item \textbf{MATH Level 5} \cite{hendrycks2021math}: The hardest tier of the MATH
    dataset. We evaluate on 50 randomly sampled problems.
\end{itemize}

We test four models spanning different capability and cost tiers:
GPT-4o, gpt-5.4-mini, gpt-5.4, and Claude Sonnet 4.5 (September 2025).

proofs. Multi-attempt generation then recovers and exceeds the original rate while maintaining
certification integrity.

\subsection{Audit: Why Z3-Only Benchmarks Are Misleading}

Manual audit of 23 ``proved'' results from the Z3-only benchmark revealed:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item 3 (13\%) \textbf{Valid}: Z3 correctly encoded and proved the claim
  \item 8 (35\%) \textbf{Partial}: Proved a simplified version or arithmetic tail
  \item 12 (52\%) \textbf{Invalid}: Vacuous encoding, assumed conclusion, or runtime crash
\end{itemize}

Common failure modes: (1) asserting the conclusion as a premise (UNSAT by construction),
(2) proving trivial arithmetic while skipping the core derivation, (3) missing imports
causing silent crashes counted as ``proved.'' This underscores the importance of proof
certificates---without them, benchmark numbers are unreliable.

\section{Case Study: Verifying an Economics Paper}
%% ============================================================

To demonstrate practical value beyond competition mathematics, we apply the full Arbiter
system---multi-agent debate with formal verification---to evaluate ``The AI Layoff Trap''
\cite{falk2026layoff}, which claims to prove that AI over-automation is inevitable and
only a Pigouvian tax can correct it.

\paragraph{Setup.} Arbiter extracted 280 claims (164 formal) from the paper, identified
10 assumptions, 17 propositions, and 7 policy recommendations. Nine specialist agents
(Industrial Organization, Macroeconomics, Public Finance, Causal Inference, Labor Economics,
plus Proponent/Skeptic/Steelman/Generalist) debated for 6 rounds across 3 providers
(gpt-5.4, claude-opus-4-6, grok-4). A validity gate with 18 rules enforced argumentative
discipline (0 violations, 8 rewrites across 54 turns).

\paragraph{Verdict.} The Skeptic won 2--1 (mean score: Skeptic 45.3 vs Proponent 39.3).

\paragraph{What survived.} All three judges agreed that the core theorem ($\alpha_{NE} >
\alpha_{CO}$ when $\eta < 1$ and $N > 1$) is mathematically correct within its model, and
that the Pigouvian tax $\tau^* = \ell(1 - 1/N)$ implements the cooperative optimum in the
baseline specification.

\paragraph{What fell.} The Proponent made 17 concessions under scrutiny:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item ``Only a Pigouvian tax'' can fix it---conceded: $\eta$-raising policies also work
  \item Repeated-game impossibility---retracted: only holds in one-shot baseline
  \item ``No retraining can slow the arms race''---conceded: $\eta$-raising shrinks the wedge
  \item Operational feasibility of $\tau^*$---conceded: requires unobservable parameters
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Key finding.} The Industrial Organization agent (24 hits, debate MVP) found a
Hotelling countermodel where $\alpha_{NE} < \alpha_{CO}$ is possible, and a folk-theorem
argument dissolving the one-shot result. The paper's formal model proves a narrow result
in a toy specification, but its claims extend to real-world policy urgency, exclusivity,
and historical inevitability---none supported by the mathematics.

\section{Discussion}
%% ============================================================

\paragraph{Why multi-backend outperforms ITP-only approaches.}
Interactive theorem provers require the LLM to generate complex tactic proofs (Lean) or
verified programs (Dafny). SMT-based approaches require only constraint encoding, which
LLMs handle well. However, raw SMT lacks proof integrity (as our audit showed). Knuckledragger
bridges this gap: it provides Z3's automation with Lean-like proof certificates. SymPy
complements it for domains (trigonometry, symbolic algebra) outside first-order logic.

\paragraph{Positioning against Lean-based SOTA.}
Kimina-Prover (92.2\%) and Goedel-Prover-V2 (90.4\%) achieve higher \emph{certified} rates
than our gpt-5.4-mini (70.8\%), but require specialized 32--72B Lean models trained on
millions of proof steps. Our approach uses general-purpose API models with no fine-tuning.
Sonnet 4.5's 89.3\% certified rate is competitive with DeepSeek-Prover-V2 (88.9\%) while
using a general-purpose model. The key advantage is accessibility: any model with structured
output support can use our pipeline, whereas Lean approaches require Lean-specific training.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
(1) Sonnet 4.5 (September 2025) is the same model used in MiniF2F-Dafny \cite{minif2f_dafny2025},
making the head-to-head fair, but is newer than models used in other comparisons.
(2) 11\% of Sonnet's proofs lack formal certificates (passed via numerical checks only).
(3) The v3 pipeline uses up to 10 LLM calls per problem; cost efficiency is lower than
single-pass approaches.
(4) On re-execution, 3/10 spot-checked proofs failed due to non-determinism in LLM
code generation---the proof code itself is correct, but regeneration may produce
different (sometimes broken) code.

\paragraph{Future work.}
Problem decomposition (breaking proofs into lemma chains before generation), domain-specific
templates, and Knuckledragger's induction tactics for recursive properties. Integration of
SageMath for advanced number theory and combinatorics.

%% ============================================================
\section{Conclusion}
%% ============================================================

We present a multi-backend approach to LLM-driven theorem proving that achieves 100\% on
miniF2F with 89.3\% machine-verified certificates, compared to 55.7\% for Dafny-based
approaches with the same model. The key insight is that combining an SMT-based proof
assistant (Knuckledragger) with a computer algebra system (SymPy) covers more mathematical
domains than either alone, while maintaining proof integrity through tamper-proof certificates.
A case study on economics paper verification demonstrates practical value: formally separating
what a paper proves from what it claims.

\paragraph{Reproducibility.}
All benchmark scripts, generated proof code, and result files are available at
\url{https://github.com/vishk23/arbiter}. The Knuckledragger library is available at
\url{https://github.com/philzook58/knuckledragger}.
