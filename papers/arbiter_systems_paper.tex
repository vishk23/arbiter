\begin{abstract}
We present Arbiter, an open-source framework that evaluates research claims through
structured multi-agent debate with formal verification. Given a PDF or research claim,
Arbiter (1) extracts formal structure (assumptions, propositions, equations, policies),
(2) assembles specialist debate agents across multiple frontier LLM providers,
(3) enforces argumentative validity via a layered gate system, and
(4) verifies quantitative claims using Knuckledragger (Z3-based proof certificates)
and SymPy (algebraic verification). In a case study on ``The AI Layoff Trap''
(Falk \& Tsoukalas, 2026), Arbiter's 9 specialist agents produced 17 author concessions
over 6 rounds, revealing that the paper's formal model proves a narrow result while its
policy claims extend far beyond the mathematics. On the miniF2F benchmark, the verification
backend achieves 244/244 (100\%) with 89.3\% machine-verified certificates using Claude
Sonnet 4.5---compared to 55.7\% for MiniF2F-Dafny with the same model. Arbiter is
11,000 lines of Python supporting 7 LLM providers, a web dashboard for live monitoring,
and exports to Argdown, Markdown, HTML, and JSON. Code and all experimental artifacts
are available at \url{https://github.com/vishk23/arbiter}.
\end{abstract}

%% ============================================================
\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%% ============================================================

Research claims regularly outrun their evidence. The replication crisis found that only
36\% of psychology studies replicate \cite{osc2015}; retraction rates have tripled to
11.2 per 10,000 papers \cite{retraction2024}, with over 9,000 retractions in 2024 alone.
A 2026 Nature study found that while 85\% of economics papers are computationally
reproducible, this only checks that code runs---not that conclusions follow from
premises \cite{nature_repro2026}. The gap between ``code runs'' and ``claims are
logically valid'' is precisely what formal verification can address.

Current AI tools for research evaluation fall into two categories that miss this gap.
\emph{Claim verification systems} (SciFact \cite{scifact2020}, PROClaim \cite{proclaim2026},
Loki \cite{loki2024}) check factual claims against retrieved evidence but do not verify
mathematical reasoning. \emph{Theorem proving systems} (DeepSeek-Prover-V2 \cite{deepseek2025},
Kimina-Prover \cite{kimina2025}, AlphaProof \cite{alphaproof2024}) verify mathematical
proofs but do not connect formal results to the broader claims a paper makes. No existing
system does both.

Arbiter fills this gap. It reads a research paper, extracts its formal structure, stages
a structured debate between specialist agents, enforces logical validity through a gate
system backed by Z3/Knuckledragger verification, and produces a verdict that distinguishes
\emph{what the paper proves} from \emph{what the paper claims}.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{System design}: An end-to-end pipeline from PDF to verdict,
    with agentic initialization, multi-provider debate, layered validity gate,
    and multi-lab judge panel (Section~\ref{sec:system}).
  \item \textbf{Formal verification backend}: Multi-backend proof generation using
    Knuckledragger (Z3 proof certificates), SymPy (algebraic verification), and raw Z3
    (counterexamples), achieving 100\% on miniF2F and 89.3\% certified
    (Section~\ref{sec:verification}).
  \item \textbf{Case study}: Evaluation of ``The AI Layoff Trap,'' revealing systematic
    overreach beyond the paper's formal model (Section~\ref{sec:case_study}).
  \item \textbf{Open-source release}: 11,000 lines of Python, 7 LLM providers, 229 tests,
    live web dashboard, full experimental artifacts.
\end{enumerate}

\section{System Architecture}
\label{sec:system}
%% ============================================================

Arbiter operates in three phases: \textbf{initialization} (PDF $\to$ debate config),
\textbf{debate} (multi-agent argumentation with validity enforcement), and
\textbf{judgment} (multi-lab verdict with score aggregation).

\subsection{Phase 1: Agentic Initialization}
\label{sec:init}

Given a PDF, Arbiter's init pipeline extracts structured content through a sequence
of LLM calls, parallelized where possible:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Claim extraction}: PDF $\to$ markdown $\to$ 50--300 typed claims
    (structural, logical, empirical, definitional, autobiographical), each tagged with
    \texttt{is\_formal} and dependency links.
  \item \textbf{Formal model extraction}: For papers with formal content, identify
    assumptions ($A_1 \ldots A_n$), propositions ($P_1 \ldots P_m$), equations, and
    policy claims, with back-references to raw claims.
  \item \textbf{Contradiction detection}: Identify logically conflicting claim pairs,
    scored by severity (fatal, tension, ambiguity).
  \item \textbf{Parallel generation} (ThreadPoolExecutor):
    \begin{itemize}
      \item Z3/Knuckledragger verification module from contradictions + formal model
      \item Specialist agent designs (system prompts, provider assignment)
      \item Judge rubric (topic-specific criteria)
      \item Validity gate rules (stipulated facts, regex patterns, test cases)
      \item Source corpus (web search + local TF-IDF index)
    \end{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Gate calibration}: Self-test the validity gate against generated gold
    cases; iteratively repair failing patterns.
  \item \textbf{Config assembly}: Write a complete YAML config (typically 200--600 lines)
    that fully specifies the debate.
\end{enumerate}

The init pipeline replaces manual config authoring. In our experiments, agentic init
produced richer configs than manual (11 agents vs 7, 14+ Z3 checks vs 3, domain-specific
rubric criteria).

\subsection{Phase 2: Structured Debate}
\label{sec:debate}

The debate engine, built on LangGraph \cite{langgraph}, orchestrates agents through
sequential rounds. Each agent turn follows the cycle:

\begin{center}
\textit{Context assembly} $\to$ \textit{LLM call} $\to$ \textit{Gate check}
$\to$ \textit{Ledger update} $\to$ \textit{Mid-debate judge signal}
\end{center}

\paragraph{Context assembly.}
Each agent receives: its system prompt (Jinja2 template interpolated with topic, Z3
stipulation, and privileged context), the debate transcript (with recency bias toward
recent rounds), and a pre-filled JSON template listing open hits it must address.
The pre-fill template places actual hit IDs at the end of the context window,
exploiting recency bias---this increased engagement from 0\% to 73\%.

\paragraph{Argument ledger.}
Agents produce structured JSON blocks containing new arguments (``hits'') and responses
to existing hits. The ledger tracks: which arguments are open (unaddressed), which have
been rebutted, and the per-agent hit balance. Convergence is detected when the ledger
stops growing for consecutive rounds.

\paragraph{Provider diversity.}
Arbiter supports 7 built-in providers (Anthropic, OpenAI, Google, xAI/Grok, DeepSeek,
Ollama, custom plugins) with a unified \texttt{BaseProvider} interface. Agents on each
debate side are distributed across providers to prevent monoculture bias---if all
Skeptic agents use the same model, they may share blind spots.

\subsection{Phase 2b: Validity Gate}
\label{sec:gate}

The validity gate enforces argumentative discipline. Its primary mode uses an LLM
classifier that checks each turn against stipulated rules:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Stipulation violations}: Asserting claims contradicted by Z3-verified facts
  \item \textbf{Definitional shifts}: Changing the meaning of key terms mid-debate
  \item \textbf{Self-contradictions}: Internal inconsistency within a single turn
  \item \textbf{Formal claim extraction}: Structural/logical claims parsed for Z3 checking
\end{itemize}

Only \emph{high-confidence} violations trigger rewrites (up to 2 per turn). In our
case study (54 turns), the gate produced 0 violations and 8 rewrites---the rewrites
improved argument quality without blocking debate flow.

The gate's value is \emph{deterrence}, not just detection. In adversarial red-team
testing, agents abandoned evasion strategies after 2 caught attempts---the gate's
existence changed agent behavior even when not triggered.

\subsection{Phase 3: Multi-Lab Judgment}
\label{sec:judge}

A panel of 3 judges (one per provider lab) independently scores the debate on a
topic-specific rubric (5--8 criteria, each 0--10). Scores are aggregated by mean;
spread exceeding a configurable threshold flags low-confidence verdicts. The verdict
includes per-criterion scores, overall winner (majority vote with score tiebreak),
and a written rationale.

\section{Formal Verification Backend}
\label{sec:verification}
%% ============================================================

Arbiter's verification backend addresses two distinct tasks:
(1) verifying quantitative claims within papers during debate, and
(2) serving as a general-purpose benchmark-grade theorem prover.

\subsection{Multi-Backend Architecture}

The system routes claims to three verification backends based on mathematical domain:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Knuckledragger} \cite{zucker2024kd,zucker2026state}: A Python proof
    assistant built on Z3 that returns tamper-proof \texttt{Proof} objects.
    \texttt{kd.prove(claim)} either produces a verified certificate or raises
    \texttt{LemmaError}---proofs cannot be faked. Handles: integer Diophantine equations,
    polynomial inequalities (QF\_NRA), divisibility, linear algebra, inductive properties
    (via \texttt{kd.Lemma + l.induct()}).
  \item \textbf{SymPy}: Computer algebra system for domains outside first-order logic.
    Uses \texttt{minimal\_polynomial(expr, x) == x} to rigorously prove algebraic zeros
    (not numerical approximation). Handles: trigonometric identities, recurrences
    (\texttt{rsolve}), series, symbolic GCD.
  \item \textbf{Raw Z3}: For counterexample extraction (\texttt{model.eval()}) and
    optimization (\texttt{Optimize}). Used as complement, not standalone prover.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Why Proof Certificates Matter}

Our initial Z3-only evaluation reported 65.7\% on miniF2F-hard, but manual audit of all
23 ``proved'' results revealed only 3 (13\%) were genuine proofs. The remaining 87\%
included vacuous encodings (asserting conclusions as premises), assumed derivations,
and runtime crashes counted as proofs. This prompted the switch to Knuckledragger,
which \emph{automatically rejects} invalid proofs via \texttt{LemmaError}.

\subsection{Generation Pipeline (v3)}

Each problem receives up to 10 LLM calls via a pipeline of:
(1) SymPy pre-computation (solve symbolically, pass answer as hint),
(2) multi-attempt generation (3 independent tries),
(3) counterexample-guided repair (extract Z3 model on failure),
(4) certificate upgrade (re-request formal certificate if only numerical).

fine-tuning. The advantage is accessibility: any model with structured output support
can use our pipeline.

\section{Case Study: The AI Layoff Trap}
\label{sec:case_study}
%% ============================================================

To demonstrate Arbiter's practical value, we evaluate ``The AI Layoff Trap''
\cite{falk2026layoff}, a widely-shared paper claiming to prove that AI over-automation
is inevitable and only a Pigouvian tax can fix it.

\paragraph{Setup.}
Arbiter extracted 280 claims (164 formal) and identified a formal model with 10
assumptions, 17 propositions, 6 equations, and 7 policy recommendations. Nine specialist
agents debated for 6 rounds across 3 providers: an Industrial Organization expert, a
Macroeconomist, a Public Finance specialist, a Causal Inference expert, a Labor Economist,
plus standard Proponent/Skeptic/Steelman/Generalist roles. The validity gate enforced
18 rules with 0 violations across 54 turns.

\paragraph{Verdict: Skeptic wins 2--1.}
Mean score: Skeptic 45.3 vs Proponent 39.3. The judges unanimously agreed that the
core theorem ($\alpha_{NE} > \alpha_{CO}$ when $\eta < 1$ and $N > 1$) is mathematically
correct. However, the Proponent made 17 concessions under scrutiny:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item ``Only a Pigouvian tax can fix it'' (Claim 12)---conceded: $\eta$-raising
    policies (education, retraining) also shrink the automation wedge
  \item Repeated-game impossibility---retracted: only holds in one-shot baseline
  \item Operational feasibility of $\tau^*$---conceded: requires unobservable parameters
  \item ``Boundless productivity'' rhetoric---conceded: not implied by the baseline model
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Key finding: the Industrial Organization agent.}
The IO specialist (24 hits, debate MVP) found a Hotelling countermodel where
$\alpha_{NE} < \alpha_{CO}$ is possible with differentiated products, a folk-theorem
argument dissolving the one-shot result, and an endogenous-entry mechanism that closes
the wedge. These are domain-specific critiques that a general-purpose LLM would not
produce without the specialist prompt design.

\paragraph{What Arbiter reveals.}
The paper's formal model proves a narrow result (over-automation wedge exists in a
symmetric, one-shot, homogeneous-good Cournot game). Its claims extend to real-world
policy urgency, historical inevitability, and intervention exclusivity---none supported
by the mathematics. Arbiter separates these two levels systematically.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
%% ============================================================

\paragraph{Claim verification.}
SciFact \cite{scifact2020} and FEVER benchmark factual claims against retrieved text.
PROClaim \cite{proclaim2026} introduces courtroom-style debate with progressive RAG,
achieving 81.7\% on Check-COVID (+10pp over standard debate). Loki \cite{loki2024}
provides open-source claim decomposition and evidence crawling. SciClaimEval
\cite{sciclaimeval2026} extends to multimodal evidence (figures, tables). These systems
verify factual claims against evidence but do not verify mathematical reasoning.

\paragraph{LLM-based peer review.}
TreeReview \cite{treereview2025} models review as hierarchical Q\&A, achieving +12\%
specificity over baselines. NeurIPS 2026 is running controlled experiments on LLM-assisted
review. At ICLR 2025, at least 15.8\% of reviews were AI-assisted
\cite{nature_review2026}. These systems assess writing quality and argumentation but
do not perform formal verification.

\paragraph{Multi-agent debate.}
Recent work questions whether debate adds value beyond majority voting
\cite{mad_scaling2025,can_llm_debate2025}. Controlled studies show debate's incremental
contribution is modest for standard reasoning tasks. DebateCV \cite{debatecv2025}
introduces Debate-SFT for claim verification. Our contribution is orthogonal:
Arbiter's value comes not from debate alone but from \emph{debate constrained by
formal verification}---Z3-verified facts serve as non-debatable stipulations that
force agents to engage with mathematical reality rather than rhetorical strategy.

\paragraph{Theorem proving.}
The Lean 4 ecosystem dominates: Kimina-Prover \cite{kimina2025} (92.2\% miniF2F),
Goedel-Prover-V2 \cite{goedel2025} (90.4\%), DeepSeek-Prover-V2 \cite{deepseek2025}
(88.9\%), BFS-Prover \cite{bfsprover2025} (73.0\%). MiniF2F-Dafny
\cite{minif2f_dafny2025} is the closest to our approach, using Dafny's Z3 backend
(55.7\% at POPL 2026). AlphaProof \cite{alphaproof2024} achieved silver-medal at
IMO 2024 (Nature 2025), while Gemini Deep Think scored gold at IMO 2025 using
natural-language reasoning \cite{gemini_imo2025}. Knuckledragger \cite{zucker2024kd}
provides Z3-based proof certificates but has not been benchmarked on standard datasets
prior to this work.

\section{Discussion and Limitations}
\label{sec:discussion}
%% ============================================================

\paragraph{When Arbiter is most useful.}
Papers with formal models (economics, game theory, CS theory) where claims extend
beyond the mathematical results. Papers with purely empirical or narrative content
benefit less from formal verification but still benefit from structured debate.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
(1) Model vintage: Sonnet 4.5 (Sep 2025) is the same as MiniF2F-Dafny but newer
than other comparisons.
(2) 11\% of miniF2F proofs lack formal certificates (numerical only).
(3) The v3 pipeline uses up to 10 LLM calls per verification problem.
(4) Debate value is questioned by recent work \cite{mad_scaling2025}; our contribution
is the formal verification constraint, not debate \emph{per se}.
(5) The gate's LLM classifier requires a capable model (gpt-5.4-mini minimum).

\paragraph{Future work.}
Progressive RAG (re-querying sources between debate rounds based on emerging arguments),
Debate-SFT (fine-tuning a small judge model on synthetic debates), integration with
SageMath for advanced number theory, and a GUI for non-technical users.

%% ============================================================
\section{Conclusion}
%% ============================================================

Arbiter demonstrates that combining multi-agent debate with formal verification
produces rigorous research evaluation that neither approach achieves alone. The system
reads a paper, extracts its formal structure, stages a structured debate, and produces
a verdict that distinguishes proven results from unsupported claims. The verification
backend achieves state-of-the-art results on miniF2F (100\%/89.3\% certified) using
general-purpose models, validating Knuckledragger + SymPy as a practical alternative
to Lean-based theorem proving. The case study on ``The AI Layoff Trap'' shows the
system's real-world value: 17 concessions revealing systematic overreach in a paper
presented as definitive proof of AI policy failure.

Arbiter is open-source at \url{https://github.com/vishk23/arbiter}.
