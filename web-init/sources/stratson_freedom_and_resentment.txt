RATIONALE: Canonical essay on responsibility, reactive attitudes, and the compatibility of moral practices with determinism.

========================================================================

Strawson’s central claim is that debates about determinism and moral responsibility often miss the real basis of our practices. In ordinary life, we do not first decide whether human beings are metaphysically free and then begin to hold one another responsible. Instead, our practices of praise, blame, gratitude, resentment, forgiveness, and indignation are rooted in the everyday interpersonal attitudes people naturally take toward one another.

He calls these attitudes "reactive attitudes." Examples include resentment when someone wrongs us, gratitude when someone benefits us, forgiveness when an offense is excused, and hurt feelings when someone shows disregard. These attitudes express our participation in ordinary personal relationships. They are not theoretical judgments about determinism; they are part of the human social fabric.

A major distinction in the essay is between the "participant" and "objective" attitudes. The participant stance is the normal stance of mutual engagement, in which we treat others as moral agents and respond to their conduct with the reactive attitudes. The objective stance is adopted when we regard someone more impersonally, perhaps because we see them as a child, a severely mentally disordered person, or someone whose behavior is not appropriately answerable to ordinary interpersonal expectations. In such cases, resentment and indignation may be tempered or replaced by management, treatment, or explanation.

Strawson argues that determinism, even if true, would not undermine these reactive attitudes or the practices built on them. Why? Because these practices are not based on a philosophical theory that assumes indeterminism. They arise from human relationships and social life. To abandon them altogether would be to withdraw from the interpersonal attitudes that make moral community possible. The issue is not whether determinism is true, but whether a person falls within the range of beings to whom these attitudes are appropriately addressed.

He also distinguishes between excuses and exemptions. Excuses show that a person’s action, though perhaps harmful, was not fully blameworthy because of special circumstances. Exemptions apply when a person is seen as lacking the kind of agency required for full participation in moral responsibility. The boundary between normal responsibility and exemption helps explain why we do not hold everyone equally accountable in all cases.

A further point is that moral responsibility practices are deeply connected to our natural human responses. Even if a philosopher were convinced that determinism is true, Strawson suggests that it would be psychologically and socially unrealistic to think we could give up resentment, gratitude, apology, love, and related attitudes. At most, we might sometimes modify them in light of special circumstances, but not eliminate them wholesale.

Overall, the essay defends the compatibility of moral practices with determinism by shifting the focus away from metaphysical freedom and toward the structure of human interpersonal life. Responsibility is grounded in the reactive attitudes that constitute our relationships, not in a speculative doctrine about causal origination.
